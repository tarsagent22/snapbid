Hiring the wrong contractor can cost you far more than the project itself. Shoddy workmanship, unexpected fees, abandoned jobs, and outright fraud happen to homeowners every day — mostly to people who didn't do their homework upfront. The good news: the vetting process isn't complicated. It just requires knowing what to check and actually checking it.

Here are 10 specific things to verify before you sign any contract with a contractor.

## 1. Verify Their State License

Most states require contractors — and especially licensed trades like plumbers, electricians, and HVAC technicians — to hold a current state license. Licensing requirements exist to protect you: unlicensed work can void your homeowner's insurance, fail inspection, and become your legal liability.

How to check: Every state has a contractor licensing board or equivalent agency. A Google search for "[your state] contractor license lookup" takes you directly to the database. Enter the contractor's name or license number and verify it's active, not expired or suspended.

Don't take their word for it — look it up yourself. Takes 60 seconds.

## 2. Confirm They Carry General Liability Insurance

If a contractor damages your property during a job — floods your basement, breaks a window, cracks a tile — their general liability insurance is what pays for the fix. Without it, you're pursuing them personally, which is difficult and expensive.

Ask for a Certificate of Insurance (COI) naming you or your address as the certificate holder. This proves coverage exists at the time of the job, not just theoretically. Call the insurance company on the certificate if you want to verify it's current.

## 3. Verify Workers' Compensation Coverage

Workers' comp protects you if a worker is injured on your property. Without it, an injured worker can potentially sue you directly as the property owner.

Any contractor with employees should carry workers' comp. Sole proprietors sometimes have exemptions depending on state law — ask about this specifically and get the answer in writing if you have concerns.

## 4. Get at Least 3 Bids

For any job over $3,000–$5,000, collecting multiple bids is the minimum standard. Three bids gives you a meaningful range. It also forces each contractor to compete on the specifics of your job rather than vague verbal estimates.

Important: make sure all bidders are quoting the same scope. If one bid includes demo and debris hauling and another doesn't, you're not comparing apples to apples. Create a simple written scope of work and give it to each contractor before they bid.

## 5. Check Reviews — the Right Way

Online reviews are useful but easy to game. Here's how to do it properly:

- **Look at Google, Yelp, and Houzz** — cross-reference across platforms
- **Read the 2–3 star reviews** — they often reveal patterns (communication issues, billing surprises, cleanup problems)
- **Check the BBB** — look for complaint history, not just the grade
- **Ask for 3 local references** from jobs similar to yours — and actually call them

When you call references, ask: "Did they stay on schedule? Did the final price match the quote? Would you hire them again without hesitation?"

## 6. Ask Who Actually Does the Work

Many general contractors subcontract all or most of the actual labor. That's normal — but you should know:

- Who specifically will be on-site daily?
- Are the subs licensed and insured?
- Will the GC be on-site to supervise, or just at the beginning and end?

The contractor you hire should be responsible for all subs they bring in. Get this in writing: your contract should state that all work will be performed by licensed, insured tradespeople.

## 7. Watch for These Red Flags

These are specific warning signs that should give you pause — or stop you entirely:

- **Asks for 50%+ upfront** — standard deposit is 10–30%, with remainder on completion
- **Only accepts cash** — no paper trail means no accountability
- **Pressures you to sign immediately** — good contractors have schedules; pressure tactics signal problems
- **No written contract offered** — if they're reluctant to put anything in writing, walk away
- **Quote dramatically lower than others** — low bids often mean something is being excluded, cut short, or not accounted for
- **No local address or no established business history** — especially common with storm chasers after weather events

> **Pro Tip:** After any major storm, your area will be flooded with out-of-state contractors offering quick fixes and demanding large deposits. Local contractors have reputation at stake. Out-of-state ones may disappear with your deposit. Always verify a local business address and license before handing over any money.

## 8. Know What a Contract Must Include

A legitimate contractor provides a written contract before work begins. That contract should specify:

- **Full scope of work** — detailed description of what's included and excluded
- **Materials** — specific brands, models, and grades (not just "tile" or "lumber")
- **Project timeline** — start date, expected completion, and milestones
- **Payment schedule** — when each payment is due and tied to what completion milestone
- **Change order process** — how scope changes are approved and priced
- **Warranty terms** — both on materials and workmanship
- **Permit responsibility** — who pulls permits and who pays for them

If a contractor hands you a one-page agreement that skips most of these, don't sign until you've added what's missing.

## 9. Understand the Payment Schedule

The payment schedule is where disputes most often begin. Here's how a legitimate payment structure typically looks:

- **Deposit (10–30%)**: Due at contract signing — covers mobilization and initial material orders
- **Progress payments**: Tied to specific milestones (e.g., rough framing complete, rough plumbing passed inspection)
- **Final payment (10–15%)**: Due only upon completion, your satisfaction, and any required final inspections passing

Never pay 100% upfront. Never let payments run ahead of work completed. If a contractor asks for more than the above structure, get a specific explanation — legitimate contractors don't need you to finance them.

## 10. Confirm Permit Responsibility

Permits aren't optional for most structural, plumbing, and electrical work — they're legally required. Unpermitted work can:

- Fail to pass inspection when you sell your home
- Void your homeowner's insurance for related claims
- Result in forced teardown and redo at your expense

Your contract should explicitly state that the contractor will pull all required permits and that all work will be inspected by the relevant authority. Ask to see permit paperwork before major work begins, and request final inspection records when the job is complete.
